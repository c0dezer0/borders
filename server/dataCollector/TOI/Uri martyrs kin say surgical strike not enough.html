      Uri martyr's kin say surgical strike not enough

      Alok K N Mishra
      People of Lali, a nondescript village nestled in forests on the outskirts of the state capital, laid Uri martyr Francis Horo (43) to rest on Sunday amidst aggressive sloganeering against Pakistan.
      | TNN | Updated: Oct 2, 2016, 10.00 PM IST

      Highlights

      • Francis Horo lost the battle for life at a hospital in New Delhi, 22 days after militants attacked an army base in Uri
      • Memories of his home visit in May came alive when his body came to the village wrapped in tri-color
      • Horo's family members feel 'surgical strike is not enough', they want revenge
      RANCHI: People of Lali, a nondescript village nestled in forests on the outskirts of the state capital, laid Uri martyr Francis Horo (43) to rest on Sunday amidst aggressive sloganeering against Pakistan. Horo is the 20th braveheart who laid his life to protect the country. His body was flown to Ranchi on Saturday evening after he lost the battle for life at a hospital in New Delhi, 22 days after militants attacked an army base in Uri (Srinagar).

      Memories of his home visit in May came alive when his body came to the village wrapped in tri-color. Prayers for the recovery of his health had begun as soon as people learnt that he was critically injured in Uri attack. "God did not answer our prayers. May be, we did not pray enough," said an elder of Horo.


      Not even minimum civic infrastructure exists in Lali, just 40km from the state capital. Bushes and forests besides huts and kuchha houses constitute the village living in sheer neglect by state. Horo nurtured his patriotism despite neglect and joined Indian Army in 1990. "We wanted him to extend his leave (in May), but he wanted return to Srinagar timely. He said Srinagar is passing through a phase of disturbance and the country needs his service," said Emil Tuti, Horo's brother.


      Full military honors marked his last rites attended by over 300 people. Some people advocated war with Pakistan others restrained their emotions. Horo's wife had no control on emotions; his elder daughter had two bouts of faintness last night. At seven, Horo's youngest of two daughters is too young to gauge the gravity of the loss.


      "He left without leaving behind a caretaker. He left behind four women (wife, mother and two young daughters) in need of care and guidance," said a villager. The life was never easy for his family, it would be very hard now.


      Elder daughter Anu Horo said only revenge of her father's death can make the family feel good. Augastasya Horo, his wife, was incoherent. "Surgical strike is not enough," she said.
